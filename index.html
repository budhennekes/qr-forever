Why this exists

Most QR generators rent you your own QR code.

Here is the trick, and it is a good one: many popular QR tools create dynamic codes. Instead of pointing to your website, the code points to their redirect server, which then forwards people to your website. That is how they track scans, and it is also the hook.

Because the moment your free trial ends or your subscription lapses, that redirect dies. The code itself is fine. It is printed on 10,000 brochures, a trade show booth, a restaurant menu, the side of a van. It just goes nowhere. Some services then offer to reactivate it, for a fee. Your printed materials become their leverage.

A static QR code is just geometry. Your link, encoded in a pattern. Nobody can turn it off, including us.

That is the whole idea behind this tool. It generates static codes only, in your browser, with the destination baked in permanently. There is no server to shut down, no account to lapse, and no company between your customer and your website. The tradeoff is honest: you do not get scan analytics, and the destination cannot be changed after printing. For most brand uses, links you control and intend to keep, that is the right trade.

Before you print

The five-point print checklist.

  • Test-scan the actual file on at least two phones before it goes anywhere near a printer. Screen first, then a test print.
  • Keep it at least 2 x 2 cm (0.8 x 0.8 in) in print. Bigger if people scan from a distance. A rough rule: scanning distance divided by 10.
  • Dark code on a light background. Inverted codes and low-contrast palettes are the number one cause of failed scans. This tool warns you when a combination is risky.
  • Leave the quiet zone alone. The empty margin around the code is part of the code. This tool includes it automatically. Do not crop it in your layout.
  • Use the SVG for print. Vector files stay sharp at any size. Hand your printer the SVG, keep the PNG for screens.

Questions

Fair questions, straight answers.

Will this code really work forever?

The code will always encode the exact link you typed. As long as that link works, the code works. There is no service in the middle that can expire, because there is no service in the middle at all.

Can I change the destination after printing?

No, and that is the tradeoff for permanence. The destination is part of the pattern. If you expect the link to change, point the code at a page you control, like yourbrand.com/menu, and update that page instead.

Do you see my links or my logo?

No. The code is generated by JavaScript running on your device. Your URL, your logo, and your downloads never touch a server. You can load this page, disconnect from the internet, and it still works.

What is the catch?

There is not one. This is a free tool built to make a point about how QR codes should work. If it is useful, share it with someone who prints things.

Why does my logo make the code look denser?

When you add a logo, we raise the code's error correction to the highest level, which adds redundancy so the code scans reliably even with part of it covered. Denser, but safer. That is the correct way to do logo codes.
